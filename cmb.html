CMB Research


During my time working for Prof. Niemack's Cosmology group, I contributed to efforts toward the next generation of Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) telescopes, including the Atacama Cosmology Telescope (ACT), CCAT-Prime, and Simon's Observatory. These telescopes aim to characterize CMB anisotropies at arcminute resolutions by surveying the sky at microwave frequencies. By doing so, we hope to accurately determine cosmological constraints while also experimentally investigating the period of inflation which occured immediately after the big bang. A secondary science goal of this work is a precise determination of neutrino masses.

My main focus during this time was building a Fourier Transform Spectrometer (FTS) for measuring anti-reflection coatings on silicon wafers to be used for telescope lenses. Our design was adapted from that of NASA's PIXIE missions (pictured above left), which aimed to probe primordial gravitational waves (Kogut, 2011). To ensure sensitivity to polarization, our design used a series of wire grids oriented orthogonally to each other as beam splitters. Interference is produced with a movable dihedral mirror mounted on a motorized stage. My work on this project encompassed both hardware and software; I designed several of the optical and mechanical components of the FTS using Zemax and Solidworks, and I also wrote control software using python/Arduino for the various motorized elements. I also helped with several liquid-helium cooldowns of the silicon bolometer detector, and assisted in streamlining this process. Currently, the FTS is being used by the group to measure wafers fabricated at the Cornell Nanoscience Facility (CNF).

A few other small projects I did during this time included designing optics tubes for CCAT-Prime and Simon's Observatory, as well as characterizing telescope beam asymmetry using gaussian beam optics models in python. Optics tubes consist of a series of lenses and lyot stops used to focus the incoming telescope beam onto the detector array; typically a telescope consists of several optics tubes. I created several optimization routines to maximize the image quality of these optics tubes by varying the lenses parameters of the aspheric lenses used in the layout while also maintaining the desired detector layout and f#. In modeling the beam asymmetry, I wrote a python program to take the FFT of a truncated guassian beam offset by some amount, and then measured the asymmetry in the FFT. The results from my program were found to be in close agreement with that of a senior research associate in our collaboration.
